# Animals Can Count and Use Zero. How Far Does Their Number Sense Go?
<p style="text-align: right"><i>Quanta Magazine, 2021</i></p>

*Jordana Cepelewicz*

[Resource on the Web](https://www.quantamagazine.org/animals-can-count-and-use-zero-how-far-does-their-number-sense-go-20210809/)

---

- some results about numerical abilities in lions, spiders, ants and frogs are cited.
- key point: an approximate sense of number seemingly evolved independently in different taxonomic families.
- the article indirectly mentions the Weber law.
- an experiment (by Rosa Rugani, post-doc at Unipd) that shows that little chickens can perform simple arithmetical abilities without training is cited.
- also Rosa Rugani showed in 2015 that animals associate small numerosities with the left and larger ones with the right.
- it is true that animals don't have the ability to count exactly, but they seem to have more than just Weber-like comparison skills based on the ANS - or at least that the ANS makes them able to do more than just inexact comparisons.
- *aside note*: research done by psychologists about animal cognition aims to answer the question "can animal (species) do this?". Making computational models of such skills aims to answer the question "how does an animal do this"?
- some recent research showed that both bees and monkeys are able to represent the zero quantity, although not symbolically, in their brains - the latter even seemingly placing it in the correct position on the number line.
Researchers involved in this study wanted to compare the neural representations of quantities in different species. So, since recording neuronal activity in bees is not so easy, they started studying crows[^kirschhock]
- > Recordings of the crows’ brain activity during these tasks revealed that neurons in a region of their brain called the pallium represent zero as a quantity alongside other numerosities, just as is found in the primate prefrontal cortex. “From a physiological point of view, this fits in beautifully,” Nieder said. “We see exactly the same responses, the same type of code, represented in the crow brain as in the monkey brain.”
- one fundamental question is what's the evolutionary need that led animals to develop an approximate sense of the quantity zero. Researchers doubt that they need this in they day-to-day activities; instead, a possibility is that numerical abilities in general first emerged as a byproduct of general visual skills (as suggested by recent computational models[^nasr][^zorzi]).

[^nasr]: [[@nasr_number_2019|Number detectors spontaneously emerge in a deep neural network designed for visual object recognition]]

authors: Marco Zorzi, Alberto Testolin
year: 2018
abstract: The finding that human infants and many other animal species are sensitive to numerical quantity has been widely interpreted as evidence for evolved, biologically determined numerical capacities across unrelated species, thereby supporting a ‘nativist’ stance on the origin of number sense. Here, we tackle this issue within the ‘emergentist’ perspective provided by artificial neural network models, and we build on computer simulations to discuss two different approaches to think about the innateness of number sense. The first, illustrated by artificial life simulations, shows that numerical abilities can be supported by domain-specific representations emerging from evolutionary pressure. The second assumes that numerical representations need not be genetically pre-determined but can emerge from the interplay between innate architectural constraints and domain-general learning mechanisms, instantiated in deep learning simulations. We show that deep neural networks endowed with basic visuospatial processing exhibit a remarkable performance in numerosity discrimination before any experience-dependent learning, whereas unsupervised sensory experience with visual sets leads to subsequent improvement of number acuity and reduces the influence of continuous visual cues. The emergent neuronal code for numbers in the model includes both numerosity-sensitive (summation coding) and numerosity-selective response profiles, closely mirroring those found in monkey intraparietal neurons. We conclude that a form of innatism based on architectural and learning biases is a fruitful approach to understanding the origin and development of number sense. This article is part of a discussion meeting issue ‘The origins of numerical abilities'.

# An emergentist perspective on the origin of number sense
<p style="text-align: right"><i>Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B: Biological Sciences, 2018</i></p>

*Marco Zorzi, Alberto Testolin*

[Resource on the Web](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstb.2017.0043)

---

-   The article discusses two hypotheses to explain the origin of the Approximate Number Sense (ANS), which take into account its innateness in different ways. The first hypothesis posits that ANS is the result of evolutionary pressure favouring individuals capable of foraging more food. The second one, that it is the result of lifelong learning of individuals exposed to visual stimuli having numerosity as a latent feature.
    
-   _p.3_ The evolutionary model shows numerosity-sensitive neurons, which are coherent with a number of other findings both in cognitive neuroscience and in cognitive modeling literature
    
-   _p.4_ The hierarchical generative model already encoded, before training, simple visuo-spatial processing mechanisms, i.e. neurons which focused on central or peripheral areas of the visual field
    
-   _p.5_ The purpose of the model is to investigate how much experience is needed to develop ANS. The initial network already shows the ability to discriminate between quantities having large numerical ratios. This was also true for models trained with very few examples.
    
-   _p.6_ Continuous visual cues (e.g. size of squares, cumulative area) influenced the accuracy of discrimination of the random network, especially when they were incongruent with the numerosity information. The mature network showed greater invariability to these cues, due to a more abstract representation of numerosity.
    
-   _p.7_ There was also assessed the presence of number coding neurons, whose firing is more correlated with a specific numerosity in the input. These have a bell-shaped activation, with peak for a specific number (especially 1 and 5, but also integers in between).
 
---

This work from Zorzi and Testolin explores thoroughly the neurobiological foundations of Approximate Number Sense (ANS), showing via computational modeling that this feature emerges from artificial networks embedded with simple visuo-spatial processing mechanisms, and improves with visual experience.

The work also explores the hypothesis that ANS could be the result of convergent evolution in many different species, caused by better fitness of individuals able to forage for more food.  
  
The two models are not in competition, in my view, and more so since the emergentist one shows that a simple ANS is already present before learning. The initial condition of the emergentist model could be the result of the evolutionary process modeled by the first one.  
  
The two models are designed to study two different phenomena (to test two hypotheses), and a model taking into account both the evolutionary perspective and the emergentist one would probably need to take into account the evolutionary pressure on neural structures in individual brains.

# How Animals Map 3D Spaces Surprises Brain Researchers

*Jordana Cepelewicz*
***

- Animals that navigate in their natural habitats[^post] create mental maps of the environment.
- One of the way in which they do so is by creating some neural codes.
- These have been discovered in rats brains in labs and might be at the basis of other forms of abstract information processing.
- Experiments with rats and bats show that information about 3D environments is encoded differently.
- The body of knowledge about the brain structures involved in spatial navigation has ? some important elements.
- > *Place cells in the hippocampus fire when an animal passes through a known location in its environment.
Head direction cells fire when the animal’s head points in a certain direction, like north or south.
Border cells fire at specific distances from a boundary.*
- Grid cells[^wiki-grid] are more sophisticated. Considering an hexagonal lattice superposed to the ground surface, grid cells fire when an animal is over any vertex of the hexagons. Different grid cells map the space at different resolutions.
- There has been evidence that these brain structures are used to map relationships between concepts as well[^cepelewicz-ideas] (cfr [[@bartlett_language_2006|Language as an Exaptation: Simulating the Origin of Syntax]]).
- There has been also evidence that these hexagonal maps of space can be distorted to give more importance to places of interest or where rewards appeared.[^cepelewicz-warp]
- In 3D spaces, the regularity of hexagonal lattice is not there. There is more of a local regularity, so that a grid cell fires in places that are approximately equidistant.
- This leads to reconsider the role of geometry in the model of grid cells in 2D spaces. The animals that have these kinds of cells probably don't build exact geometrical maps of spaces, especially in complex natural environments, but rather build approximate relationships between points in the space.
- This surely means that models of path integration, i.e. estimating one's position relative to a starting point without the support of external cues, need to be revised, since they assumed perfect hexagonal mapping of space.
- One new model ? that 3D space is mapped in the brain using the same grid cells as 2D spaces, each cell creating a 2D map of a different plane intersecting the space.
- The hippocampus is the place in the brain where information from different sources are conveyed to build representations of past experiences and general knowledge.
- One of the sources of information is the entorhinal cortex which contains the grid cells, so the new findings about grid cells could impact also our current understanding of memory processes.
- The fact that in 2D grid cells are able to build almost exact representations of space shows that maybe 2D space is privileged compared to 3D (bats and fishes have preferred heights and depths to move at). Also, this could be a sign of evolutionary processes showing structures that have evolved to serve a purpose (navigating in 2D) and have been co-opted to solve a different kind of problem (navigating in 3D and more).

title: How to Make a Class
authors: Matteo Pasquinelli
year: 2021
abstract: It was not a cybernetician but a neoliberal economist who provided the first systematic treatise on connectionism or, as it would later be known, the paradigm of artificial neural networks. In his 1952 book The Sensory Order, Friedrich Hayek advanced a connectionist theory of the mind already far more advanced than the theory of symbolic artificial intelligence, whose birth is redundantly celebrated in 1956 with the exalted Dartmouth workshop. In this text Hayek provided a synthesis of Gestalt principles and considerations of artificial neural networks, even speculating about the possibility of a machine fulfilling a similar function of “the nervous system as an instrument of classification,” auguring what we call today a “classifier algorithm.” This article shows how Hayek’s connectionist theory of the mind was used to shore up a specific and ideological view of the market and schematically reconstructs Hayek’s line of argumentation from his economic paradigm backward to his theory of cognition. Eventually, in Hayek’s interpretation, connectionism provides a relativist cognitive paradigm that justifies the “methodological individualism” of neoliberalism.

# How to Make a Class
[Resource on the Web](https://read.dukeupress.edu/qui-parle/article/30/1/159/173822/How-to-Make-a-ClassHayek-s-Neoliberalism-and-the)

> While Donald Hebb was a neuropsychologist [...] Hayek was an economist who studied the self-organization of the mind in a similar way but to support a political belief, namely, the spontaneous order of markets.

- In the mid-twentieth century, during and shortly after the Second World War, the theory of self-organization in computing networks and that of self-organization in markets contributed to the reciprocal development. This happened in a very rich and interesting period in world economics when Keynesian theories clashed with the arising neoliberal doctrines (of Hayek, among the others).

> Just as much as the decentralized topology of the Arpanet military network (the precursor of the internet) was designed as a reaction to Soviet military threat, Hayek’s connectionism was conceived, among other stimuli, as a response to socialist centralized planning and Keynesianism.

## The Decentralized and Tacit Rationality of the Market
- In the 1930s economists were debating about the possibility to establish the optimal price of good in specific economic systems (such as the socialist centralized ones). Hayek intervened in this debate framing the problem of establishing optimal prices as a problem of decentralized knowledge management:

> It is rather a problem of how to secure the best use of resources known to any of the members of society, for ends whose relative importance only these individuals know. (cit. Hayek)

> Hayek’s idea that the market is a place of distributed knowledge did not proceed from the study of economic phenomena but was first extrapolated from holistic neurology and early theories of neuroplasticity.

> Alongside the decentralization of knowledge, in his economic paradigm Hayek performed another important operation of decentering: the mobilization of tacit knowledge.

> The “know how” consists in the capacity to act according to rules which we may be able to discover but which we need not be able to state in order to obey them.

- Hayek, borrowing from others work, stressed the importance of the implicit rules, conventions and "knowledge" that is collective and shared in a group of people, giving it the fancy name of "supra-conscious" order.

> What escapes Hayek’s assessment is that this decentralized and unconscious rationality is not only of markets but can be found in other forms of human organization and cooperation.

> Hayek could not forecast that, at the turn of the coming century, digital networks and large data centers, employing the very artificial neural networks discussed by cyberneticians, would be able to trace and compute social behaviors and collective rationality in real time, inaugurating a highly effective regime of knowledge extractivism on a global scale.

## The Faculty of Classification; or, What Is a Pattern?
- Hayek provides in The Sensory Order some examples of mechanical machines that can show how the process of classification in the mind works (according to him). These machines "produce classes" in the sense that they group elements that produce the same effect when they are "input" (or appear) in the system.

> This means that for Hayek (as for the cyberneticians), the establishment of a class is a pragmatic gesture rather than an abstract one, much like the acquisition of an individual habit or social convention by repetition. For Hayek, different perceptual events are recognized as part of the same class whenever they trigger, in all their instances, the same effect in the nervous system or as motor response [...]

> Gestalt theory had a profound influence on Hayek, to the extent that his theoretical framework can be considered the translation of Gestalt principles in the economic and social field.

> Already in Sensory Order he expanded the understanding of pattern beyond the visual sphere and in so doing covered, respectively, “patterns within the brain,” “topological patterns,” “patterns of movements,” “temporal patterns,” “patterns of behavior,” “patterns of motor responses,” “patterns of attitude or dispositions,” “patterns of nervous impulses,” and so on.

> Like other modern philosophers, Hayek made no distinction between the ability to invent a class and to change behavior: the constitution of habits and norms follows the same logic as the constitution of ideas.

>  [...] The disruption of traditional and familiar classes through which reality is perceived and the reconstitution of new ones within unexpected constellations should be considered the *modus operandi* of science: [...] «The whole history of modern Science proves to be a process of progressive emancipation from the innate classification of the external stimuli till in the end they completely disappear.»

## The Connectionist Machine as Model of the Mind
> Hayek argued that the mind [...] can only provide a model rather than a representation of the world. 
> [...] The construction of a model is the implementation of a given environment within the internal and constraints of another environment, yet in the process of translation some elements are dispersed, approximated and distorted.

> The theoretical difference and historical gap between Babbage and Hayek mirror the difference between symbolic and connectionist AI, between an idea of cognition based on representation and one based on modeling.

## The Market as a Neural Network
> In addition to the theory of pattern recognition, Hayek is acknowledged for having employed *avant la lettre* a technical definition of information.

- Hayek argued that the market worked like "a kind of machinery for registering change, or a system of telecommunications", the closest images he could dispose of at the time to refer to what we would call a computing system today.
In particular, he thought of something similar to an ANN, a computing in which processing units receiving only partial information about the state of the system perform simple calculations to lead to an equilibrium outcome. 

> It is more than a metaphor to describe the price system as a kind of machinery for registering change, or a system of telecommunications which enables individual producers to watch merely the movement of a few pointers [...] in order to adjust their activities to changes of which they may never know more than is reflected in the price movement.

- A similar metaphor was used by a socialist economist, some years later, to deal with the troubled interaction with spontaneity in the market and state control:
> Implicitly, Lange suggested the use of the computer as technical mediator between the troubles of market spontaneity and (the troubles of) centralized planning.

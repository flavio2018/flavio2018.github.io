Maybe the earliest example of developement of written symbols for numbers is given by the sumerian tokens system.  
  
At the dawn of agricultural societies, when trade and taxation systems were created and flourished, sumerians used to represent units of traded goods with small pieces of clay, named by modern archaeologists 'tokens'.  
  
A token could be plain (a sphere, a cilinder, a cone) or complex (a more sophisticated three-dimensional object, with a carved surface), depending on the kind of goods it represented.  
  
Both kinds of tokens were used for accounting purposes. Complex tokens were perforated and strung to a bigger piece of clay, called 'bulla'. Plain tokens, instead, were stored inside a bigger, hollow sphere of clay.  
  
In order to count the number of tokens contained in a sphere, clerks started to impress the negative of the tokens contained therein on its surface.  
Finally, the hollow spheres were abandoned, and the tokens were used as stamps on smaller tablets of clay to record the quantity of goods.

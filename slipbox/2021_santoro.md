    

Symbolic Behaviour in Artificial Intelligence
---
-   _p.2_ The aim of the paper is to investigate symbolic manipulation abilities from a behavioral point of view. To this end, the authors define symbols emphasizing their conventional and interpretative nature, and then characterize symbolic systems starting from this definition.
    
-   _p.3_ A symbol is composed of a substrate and a meaning assigned by convention.
    
-   _p.3_ The authors build upon Charles S. Pierce’s notion of icons (resamble something), indexes (change in agreement with spatio-temporal variation of something else) and symbols (have meaning due to convention, disregarding their form or spatio-temporal change).
    
-   _p.5_ Some consequences of the conventional assignment of meaning to symbols: 
    
    -   they only have meaning with respect to some “coding scheme”; 
        
    -   they can have meaning wrt a system but not to another; 
        
    -   their meaning should be understood as an “holistic property” of the entire symbolic system, in that it is established in relation with other symbols within it.
        
-   _p.6_ The authors define symbolic behaviour to be: receptive, constructive, embedded, separable, meaningful, graded.
    
-   _p.6_ Receptiveness is defined as the ability to recognize and use a new symbol, once its meaning has been stated, knowing that other agents understand the symbol as well. Much research is ongoing in this area.
    
-   _p.6_ Constructiveness is defined as the ability of a model to assign meaning to a substrate previously meaningless. There is scarce evidence of this ability in current models. Some possible examples of evidence of such ability are proposed by the authors.
    
-   _p.7_ Embeddedness is a property which directly follows from the third consequence of conventionality of meaning stated in _p.5_. It is defined as the property of a symbol’s meaning to be dependent on the other symbols in the system, and to be able to influence their meaning as well. Embodiment, as proposed by McClelland among others, is thought to possibly improve this ability.
    
-   _p.8_ Malleability can be seen as a direct consequence of Constructiveness. It can be defined as the capacity to assign new meaning to a symbol. The type of malleability currently showed by models is passive, as it is a consequence of learning.
    
-   _p.8_ Separability refers to the capacity to distinguish between multiple meanings of a symbol, between the symbol and its substrate, and to assign a new meaning to a symbol, even temporarily.
    
-   _p.9_ Meaningfulness is the property of understanding the meaning of symbols (and not only the syntax behind them) and to use such understanding to reason and explain one’s reasoning.
    
-   _p.10_ Gradedness refers to the fact that symbolic manipulation is an ability which humans can possess not completely, wrt one or more of the features stated above.
    
-   _p.10_ The author highlight that purely syntactic manipulation of symbols cannot be considered symbolical behavior, since the fundamental assumption that the system itself is aware to be manipulating symbols belonging to a system is taken for granted.
    
-   _p.11_ An interesting point of view about ethics in rule-based AI system is proposed, arguing that such systems would not be capable to be ethical because our ethical principles are not strict, but very context-dependent, thus requiring the understanding of the rules, and not mere application.
    
-   _p.12_ Building on work from several researchers, including Tomasello, the authors argue that symbolic behaviour might emerge as a result of social interactions in which agents must reconcile individual perspectives at first on some “objective truth”, and later on no objective common ground at all, as it can happen with human language.
    
-   _p.15_ The authors criticize a view in which symbolic behaviour is thought to emerge exclusively from internal thought processes of an agent.

abstract: Mathematical abilities, as any other task that requires capacity of abstraction, are considered a distinctive human trait. Complex mathematical concepts are built upon one apparently simple and basic operation counting. Children in all schools of the world learn to count, and our prehistorical ancestors invented this behavior probably to solve practical problems. I have built a computational model of counting behavior, trying to investigate how it might emerge as a consequence of computational constraints imposed on the input, learning signal and model architecture. In particular, I have trained two deep reinforcement learning models in two different experimental settings, consisting in learning environments where the items to be enumerated were presented either spatially or temporally to the RL agent. The experimental evidence collected under the first experimental setting revealed its intrinsic limitations as a cognitive model of human learning. The second experimental setting led to more relevant findings, which can be read under the light of recent discussions about the emergence of symbolic behavior in artificial intelligence models.

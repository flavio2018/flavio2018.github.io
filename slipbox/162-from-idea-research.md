# From idea to publishable research
<p style= "text-align: right"><i>Created 24/10/2021</i></p>

Scientific research papers are a very specifoc kind of manuscripts written according to a practices established over time in the scientific community.

Specifically, way more often than not, scientific papers present experimental results (when they don't they build narratives, theories) supporting some specific *claims* that are in turn inserted into a *narrative* that is somewhat related to the current theoretical framework about the problem at hand.

Therefore, the main work that is behind the publishing of scientific papers (at least from my current perspective of a PhD student) is the design and carrying out of experiments, i.e. the production of the experimental evidence which the claims can be based upon.

In the fields I will do research in, producing experimental evidence is done by means of computational simulations (in computational cognitive neuroscience) or simply training and benchmarking (in machine learning).

A general problem in experimental sciences is designing experimental settings. This problem is a meta-knowledge problem, since it is about creating the conditions under which experimental evidence (on which claims are based) can be created.

It can happen in general in other experimental sciences that creating  successful and efficient experimental settings can take years.

In the case of computational sciences, a part of building experimental settings is doing technical work - i.e. writing the programs that will enable the running of experiments and partially constitute the experiments themselves[^wrapup].

Starting from topics that are of interest for someone, how can experimental results that can be of interest for the community (and therefore be published) be produced?

One way is surely knowing a whole body of well established methods that are present in the literature and are used in the community to produce experimental evidence.

This way, when one idea about possible research questions pops into one's mind, with experience one can relatively easily think about ways in which that question can be investigated or answered.

One other key ingredient towards producing interesting and publishable experimental results is knowing the current state of the debate in the field that the research would be inserted into.

One more important and necessary piece of an effective experimental design is domain knowledge. Indeed, in some fields more than others, being familiar with existing concepts and techniques can save a lot of time - when not allowing the research to be carried out entirely.

This means that one can realistically be able to follow that many areas of research and master the relative background knowledge well enough to be able to think, design and carry out experiments in those areas.

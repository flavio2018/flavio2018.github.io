*Andrej Karpathy*

***

- understand how the supervisor position works: what are their goals, the way in which they get funds, the ways in which they get recognition by colleagues, in general what motivates them and drives them. Key goal: avoid friction and plan appropriately.
- the position of the supervisor is in also influences the kind of feedback they will be able to give. Most important thing: feedback is necessary, seek for it as much as possible. "Post-tenure" can give more high-level feedback, while "pre-tenure"/post-doc/older PhD can give a more low-level feedback on ideas/code/maths.
- possible differences:
	- kind of relationship: more or less formal
	- influence details of work or not
	- focus on methods or focus on tasks
	- meet every day/week/month
	- answer emails fast or slow
	- control over work schedule or not
	- funds equipment (having the money) or not
	- funds conferences trips or not
	- is ok with summer internships or not
	- practical vs theoretical work
- the nature of the PhD is two fold, or has a meta-level, or again has two nested loops: what problems do I solve and how do I solve the problems
- reasoning about the outer loop (what problems do I try to solve) one gets to develop a "thing", a sense of taste for good problems, based on how well they are posed, how solvable they could be or what's their general appeal
- some of the things that go into this taste: a sense of the available room for additional work and improvement. Get on someone's (your adviser's) train: get what their core interests are and follow that path; this way they'll be more likely to help and your work will be promoted by them.
- choosing a topic: one should choose something big enough (you want to be remembered), but a at the same time that is approachable, that "has an attack".
- another thing that one should consider when choosing the topic of study is the skills you will acquire working on it. Will they be valuable skills?
- it's in general bad practice to do "incremental work", i.e. experiments that are small obvious improvements to some existing work, that are easily accepted in conferences because they are hard to criticize on the matter of the paper
- review papers to write good papers. Papers "are a thing", they have their own "literary style". Only examining good papers, however, doesn't put you in the condition to see how a bad paper is written. So, you can do review for conferences (can you?). Another good experience are journal clubs, where experienced researchers critique other researcher's papers.
- papers even have a gestalt, an expected form that can be a cognitive shortcut that others use to judge them.
- papers should be about one specific, novel and important contribution in the field, supported with strong experimental evidence and surrounded by the necessary context (no more, no less words).
The paper should be seen as equivalent to a scientific claim about something. It should be possible to summarize its content in one sentence, which should be its title. The content of the paper itself should be, then, the body of work and experimental evidence, both done by the authors and not, that led to make that claim. 
- > *The upper level structure is by default Intro, Related Work, Model, Experiments, Conclusions.* ... *A good flow of ideas is then along the lines of 1) X (+define X if not obvious) is an important problem 2) The core challenges are this and that. 2) Previous work on X has addressed these with Y, but the problems with this are Z. 3) In this work we do W (?). 4) This has the following appealing properties and our experiments show this and that.*
- in a paper not everything you did should be reported and everything that is reported should be motivated.
> *[The paper] is a highly processed and very focused discussion of a problem, your approach and its context. It is supposed to teach your colleagues something and you have to justify your steps, not just describe what you did.*
- internal deadline: 2 weeks before final deadline, with "at least a 5-page draft with all the final experiments (even if not with final numbers)".
- some more stuff on attending and speaking at conferences that are not immediately interesting for me right now.

## Introduction
- > *Culture can be defined as a body of information acquired from other members of the population via teaching, imitation, or other forms of social interaction, which is capable of affecting the behavior of individuals*
- "cumulative culture" seems to be a well established notion in the field of studies on culture
- some researchers mainly in the 90s argued that the contemporary complex human culture has been a product of genetic mutation occurred 50 kya, causing a mutation in the brain structure of early modern humans.
- This hypothesis has been challenged by a number of archaeological discoveries that show the occurrence of behaviors possibly resulting from modern cognitive processes well before 50 kya.
- This finding supports a new view according to which some genetic modification defining our species did happen, however it allowed early humans to develop cumulative cultures.
- However, if this hypothesis were true, we should not observe similar signs of behavior caused by modern cognitive processes outside of Africa (where *Homo Sapiens* first emerged) before the spreading of our species outside the continent.
- This is in contrast with several finding suggesting that Neanderthals were already engaging in symbolically mediated behavior, before the contact with anatomically modern humans.
- > *The above evidence ... suggests that genes and culture coevolved for much longer than may have been expected, and that they interacted in ways still unknown to us.*
- There has been some modeling work in the last two decades investigating about the impact of several exogenous factors (social/demographic and ecological/climatic) on the emergence of cultural innovations.
- The paper can be considered complementary to the researches above in that it investigates the *endogenous* factors leading to cultural innovations, rather than the exogenous ones.
- > *Heyes ... argues in favor of the idea that most cognitive processes enabling cumulative cultural evolution—social learning, imitation, teaching, social motivation, theory of mind, and, in particular, reading—are essentially cultural in nature and did not stem from genetic evolution.*

## Cultural exaptation
- One of the key points of D'Errico is the exaptation of utilitarian cultural features for non-utilitarian, aesthetic or symbolic purposes (e.g. ochre).

## Cultural neural reuse
- > *The notion of neural reuse has recently been introduced to indicate that for a new cognitive  function to emerge it is not always necessary to evolve new specific brain areas.* 
- This happens provided that the new region has the information processing capabilities of the reused area are sufficient.
	- How does one measure the information processing capabilities of one brain area?
- Neural reuse can be considered as "normal" biological exaptation[^gould].
- *Cultural* neural reuse refers to the specific case in which brain regions change purpose as a consequence to the exposure to some cultural practice.
- The authors propose four necessary traits: the neural reuse must be triggered by cultural practices, they must not be direct consequence of some heritable modification of the genome, they must result in new functional or structural brain networks, and the brain area should be used for a different purpose than the one it evolved for.
	- how can connections in an artificial neural network be compared to connections in the human brain?[^simulated]
- Neuroplasticity - i.e. the change in neural connections in brains - is something that always happens with any kind of learning, such as perceptual or motor learning. In regular cases, existing networks are strengthened by learning; in cultural neural reuse *new* networks are formed.
- Brain networks can either be structural or functional. Structural or anatomical brain networks are composed of brain regions that are physically connected by fiber pathways. Functional networks are instead brain regions that show patterns of co-activation.
- Cultural neural reuse therefore refers to cases in which either functional or structural brain networks are formed during an individual's lifespan as a result of cultural practices. These networks are not heritable.
- Interestingly, research in the last two decades has provided a set of diagnostics to establish with reasonable certainty whether a manufact was used as an Artificial Memory System or not. Two distinctive traits are that the objects must show signs of repeated use over time in distinct sessions (updating information) and signs of consumption due to manipulation and handling.
- D'Errico offers a list of several prehistorical artifacts that are now considered AMS. He also specifies whether the information was presumably retrieved from these objects visually or touching them.
	- In both cases, how could the users of these AMS retrieve exact information about numerosity without counting, i.e. without having words for numbers? Did they not extract exact information? Did the storing of information then differ from the original source of information only because it was permanent in time and space? Yet, the procedural nature of marking is very close to that of counting, except number words refer to *concepts* and are accumulators. Aren't concepts just another kind of tool that we learned to use *together with* other (material) tools[^sharpness]?
- An hypothesis advanced by D'Errico is that, since the tools and techniques used to produce AMS are the same as the ones used to produce other kind of decorative objects, and decorative habits were in place before AMS were used, maybe the advent of AMS was an instance of cultural exaptation of other kinds of practices.
- D'Errico also makes another point: the use of decorative elements on one's body or clothes already requires to associate that decorative element (e.g. color or shape) with some "meaning" (e.g. social status or kin). This kind of association is similar to that which one could do when associating a mark with an item using an AMS.

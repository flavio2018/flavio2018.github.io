You begin to think _with_ the interface, learning patterns of thought that would formerly have seemed strange, but which become second nature. The interface begins to disappear, becoming part of your consciousness. You have been, in some measure, transformed.*

## What makes an interface transformational?
- Nielsen calls such interfaces *transformative*
- Language is a transformative interface, thinking about the moments of our lives when we first started learning and using it and it made possible for us to express more complex ourselves using it.
- Concepts introduced: 
	- *cognitive technology*: "an external artifact, designed by humans, which can be internalized, and used as a substrate for cognition"
	- *elements of cognition*: the individual pieces the technology is made of (words, phrases, ...) which we can think with.
- even visual thinking is a form of cognitive technology - we usually perceive the world visually according to standardized visual concepts that we learned; artists *literally* learn to see the world in a different way
- Esempio della mappa della metro di Londra, come Baricco

## How can we invent new elements of cognition?

### Heuristic 1: Reify hidden representations in the interface
- a point about teaching: Nielsen recognizes a gap between what is taught to students and what professionals and experts in any field (mathematics, engineering, ...) actually do and the way they think to solve problems
- what Nielsen suggests is that ways in which experts think about things - the mental tools they used - are reified into tools, educational material, etc.
	- this could tell us something also about the way in which mathematical tools were invented - following the intuitions of creative individuals that then teach others and spread new ways of thinking
- a tool that Nielsen believes experts use is *minimal canonical examples*, that are a kind of hidden representations. A key point about these is that they allow experts to do quick, *approximate* reasoning, based on other background knowledge that they already posses.
- Just about next there is an interesting point about the difference between human mathematical abilities and the abilities of mathematical solving software to perform *exact reasoning* (i.e. purely syntactical manipulation)!
- one reason Nielsen thinks hidden representations not always end up creating new tools for thought for everybody is their distance from the traditional tools to build representations.

### Heuristic 2: Reify deep principles about the world in the interface
- Nielsen suggests that one heuristic that should guide us when creating interfaces is to embed deep principles in the interface. By deep principles he means theories about some part of the world; he makes a couple of physics examples.
- one interesting consequence of this is that mastering the interface requires mastering the underlying knowledge. This is interesting because it is an example of the positive effect of materialising knowledge. Normally, mastering knowledge (e.g. studying) would be seen as a purely theoretical, out-of-thin-air process. Instead, thinking about embedding concepts in artifacts, i.e. building artifacts that work according to the principles expressed by those concepts, is something that highlights the importance of our interaction with material or virtual objects[^games].
	- it's quite ironic that it was a game to give Nielsen the idea about this principle

## Conclusion
- one common way to think about the augmentation to our experience that computers can offer is *cognitive outsorucing*, i.e. delegating the solution to a cognitive problem to the computer, much like we delegate physical work to other kinds of machines.
- another way to think about the augmenting abilities that computers have (much like other tools) is that of allowing new ways of thinking, to extend the range of the conceivable.
